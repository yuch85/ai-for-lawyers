What LLMs Can and Can't Do

Jess: Welcome back to AI for Lawyers. We've covered what AI is and how large language models work under the hood. Today is the practical episode — where should you trust these tools, and where should you absolutely not?

Dan: this is the one I've been waiting for. Because I think a lot of lawyers fall into one of two camps — either they're afraid to use AI at all, or they're using it without really understanding where the risks are.

Jess: both of those positions are dangerous, honestly. So let's start with the good news — the things these models genuinely do well. The "can do" list is actually quite impressive for legal work.

Dan: Okay, give me the highlight reel.

Jess: Drafting is the obvious one. First drafts of memos, emails, client updates, contract clauses — the model is remarkably good at producing coherent, well-structured text. Summarising is another strength. Hand it a fifty-page judgment and ask for a two-page summary, and the result is usually solid. It's also excellent at reformatting — take a wall of text and turn it into bullet points, a table, or a structured outline.

Dan: what about comparing documents? I spend a lot of time reviewing redlines.

Jess: comparison is a sweet spot. Give it two versions of a contract and ask what changed, and it will identify the differences with impressive accuracy. It's also strong at brainstorming — generating ideas, angles, counterarguments. And explaining complex concepts in simpler language. If you need to explain a technical legal principle to a non-lawyer client, these tools are genuinely helpful.

Dan: So the pattern is — anything that involves working with text that's already in front of it, it tends to do well.

Jess: That's a great way to frame it. The model excels when the answer is in the input. Now let's talk about where it falls apart. And this is where lawyers get into serious trouble.

Dan: The "can't do" list.

Jess: Number one — and this cannot be overstated — factual accuracy, especially citations. The model will confidently generate case citations that do not exist. It will fabricate statute numbers. It will invent holdings. And it will do all of this in perfectly formatted legal prose that looks completely legitimate.

Dan: That's terrifying. Why does it do that?

Jess: remember what we discussed last episode — it's a pattern-completion engine. It knows what a case citation looks like. It knows the pattern of "In the matter of X versus Y, the court held that..." So it generates text that follows that pattern. But it's not consulting a database of real cases. It's predicting what words would most plausibly come next. And sometimes the most plausible-sounding citation is one that doesn't exist.

Dan: this is the hallucination problem.

Jess: Exactly. the critical thing to understand is that the model cannot tell you when it's hallucinating. It has no internal mechanism to flag uncertainty. It doesn't think, "I'm not sure about this citation, let me note that." It simply generates the most probable text. The confidence level in the output is always the same, whether the content is accurate or completely fabricated.

Dan: there have been real consequences for lawyers who didn't verify, haven't there?

Jess: There have. Courts have sanctioned lawyers for submitting briefs containing AI-generated citations that turned out to be entirely fabricated. These weren't obscure cases — they made headlines and became cautionary tales across the profession. The lawyers involved trusted the output because it looked right. The formatting was correct. The case names sounded plausible. But when the court or opposing counsel checked, the cases simply did not exist.

Dan: What's the lesson there? Because I imagine those lawyers weren't trying to deceive anyone. They just didn't verify.

Jess: That's exactly the point. Intent doesn't matter when you've filed a brief citing non-existent authority. The professional obligation to verify your work product doesn't disappear because a machine generated the first draft. And here's the analogy I keep coming back to — you would never file a brief that a junior associate drafted without reading it yourself. You wouldn't submit a contract your paralegal prepared without reviewing every clause. The same standard applies to AI-generated work.

Dan: So the AI is the junior, and you're still the supervising partner.

Jess: Precisely. And let me add a few more things to the "can't do" list. Mathematics — these models are unreliable with calculations. Don't use them for financial analysis or damages computations without independent verification. Real-time information — the model's training data has a cutoff date. It doesn't know about yesterday's court decision or last week's regulatory change. And professional judgment — the model cannot weigh competing considerations the way a practised lawyer can. It can't assess litigation risk. It can't read the room in a negotiation.

Dan: so what's the framework? How should a lawyer think about when to use these tools and when not to?

Jess: here's my simple rule. Use AI for the first draft, never the final product. Use it for tasks where the answer is in the input — summarising, reformatting, comparing, brainstorming. Be deeply sceptical when it generates facts, citations, or numbers that you haven't independently verified. And always — always — apply the same standard of review you would apply to any work product from any source.

Dan: That's the verification imperative.

Jess: That's exactly what it is. The tool has changed. The professional responsibility hasn't. LLMs are powerful drafting and thinking tools. They are not research oracles. They are not substitutes for legal judgment. They are the most capable junior associate you've ever worked with — and like every junior associate, their work needs to be checked. Next time, we'll look at the AI ecosystem — the different models, tools, and platforms available, and how to tell them apart.
