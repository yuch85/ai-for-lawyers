Tara: And that's the problem. Because they are very different things, even when they feel similar. So let me start with the most important concept in this entire episode. A large language model — at its core — is just text in, text out. That's it. You give it text, it gives you text back. Everything else you see when you use an AI tool? That's software engineering wrapped around that core function.

Dan: okay, so the model itself is actually quite simple in concept?

Tara: Conceptually, yes. The engineering behind it is extraordinarily complex — we covered that in episode two. But from a user's perspective, the model is a text-prediction engine. Those are the three big commercial offerings.

Dan: Are there others?

Tara: there are also open-weights models — released by companies like Meta and Mistral from the West and Chinese models from Alibaba and Moonshot — that anyone can download and run on their own hardware. These matter for firms exploring self-hosted solutions where data never leaves the building. But whether the model is open or proprietary, most lawyers will interact with the model through a commercially offered product built on top of them.

Dan: Built on top of them — what do you mean by that?

Tara: This is the big idea. Nothing you interact with is the raw model. Every product is a wrapper around the model. And wrappers vary dramatically in what they add. Think of it this way — you've never talked to a raw model. You've always talked to software that talks to the model on your behalf.

Dan: that's an interesting way to put it. So even something as simple as ChatGPT — that's a wrapper?

Tara: Absolutely. And even a quote-unquote "simple" chat interface is surprisingly complex under the hood. Between your prompt and the raw model, there are multiple invisible layers. Safety filters that screen for harmful content. System instructions that shape the model's tone and behaviour. Reinforcement tuning that teaches it to be helpful rather than just completing text. Content policies that govern what the model will and won't do.

Dan: so when I type something into ChatGPT, it's not going straight to the model?

Tara: Not even close. You see a text box. Underneath that text box is a substantial piece of software engineering. Your input gets processed, augmented, filtered — and then it reaches the model. The model's output then goes through another set of filters before it reaches you. And here's the important implication — this is why the same underlying model can behave differently in different products. The wrapper is different.

Dan: So if I use GPT through ChatGPT versus through some other tool, I might get different responses even though it's the same model underneath?

Tara: Exactly. Different system instructions, different safety filters, different post-processing. and there's something else worth noting for lawyers specifically — the provider is actively processing your input through multiple layers before and after the model sees it. That's something to keep in mind when we talk about confidentiality and data security later in the series.

Dan: That's a really important point. So my prompt doesn't just go to the model — it passes through the provider's infrastructure.

Tara: Right. Now let's move up the complexity ladder. Using Copilot in Microsoft Word is a more complex wrapper. It reads your document, constructs a prompt behind the scenes, sends it to the model, and applies the result back into your document. You never see the prompt it actually sends to the model.

Dan: so the AI isn't just responding to what I type — it's responding to a prompt that the software constructed from my document?

Tara: Precisely. The wrapper is doing significant work — deciding what context to include, how to frame the request, how to format the response back into your document. You're interacting with the wrapper, not the model. now go one step further. Legal-specific tools like Harvey add even more layers. Connections to legal databases. Citation verification. Specialised prompting logic. Workflow automation. Jurisdictional awareness. These are complex software products that happen to use an LLM as one component.

Tara: Exactly that. Each layer adds capability, but it also adds design decisions, limitations, and cost. And here's the key question when you're evaluating any AI tool — what model is underneath, and what has the wrapper added? Because you're paying for the wrapper, not the model.

The wrapper is the entire car — steering, brakes, navigation, seatbelts. No one would strap themselves to a bare engine and call it transport. The wrapper isn't packaging. It's what makes the thing usable and safe.
