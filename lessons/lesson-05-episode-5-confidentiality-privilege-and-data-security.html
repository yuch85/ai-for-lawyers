These come with contractual commitments that your data won't be used for training. Stronger access controls. Data residency options — meaning your data stays in a specific jurisdiction. Audit rights. Zero data retention or ZDR. The legal protections are in the contract, not in a consumer terms of service that nobody reads.

Dan: and this isn't a technical distinction — it's a legal one?

Tara: Exactly. The same model can sit behind both tiers. The same GPT or the same Claude. The technical infrastructure might be nearly identical. The difference is the contractual wrapper around it.

Dan: so in practice, should a lawyer be thinking about which tier of tool to use for different types of information?

Tara: Exactly. Think of your information in tiers. Some things are genuinely public — published legal texts, general legal questions, non-sensitive administrative tasks. A free tool is fine for those. Then there's internal material — your own work notes, legal research, case law summaries — where you'd want at least some confidence the provider isn't training on your input. And then there's confidential client information — names, deal terms, privileged communications — where you need enterprise-grade contractual protections or you simply don't use the tool. The higher the sensitivity of the information, the stronger the contractual protections need to be around the tool you use it with. And that contractual wrapper is now, as of early this year, a privilege question.

Dan: You're talking about the Heppner case.

Tara: United States v. Heppner, Southern District of New York, February 2026. Judge Rakoff. this is the case that changed the conversation for every lawyer working with AI. Let me walk through the facts.

Dan: Please.

Tara: Heppner was a criminal defendant charged with securities fraud. After receiving a grand jury subpoena, he used the free consumer version of Claude to prepare defence strategy documents. He input information he'd received from his lawyers — privileged communications — into the tool. He then shared the AI-generated documents with his counsel. The FBI subsequently seized his devices and found these documents.

Dan: I can already see where this is going.

Tara: Judge Rakoff held that the documents were protected by neither attorney-client privilege nor the work product doctrine. On privilege — the communications were with an AI tool, not with an attorney. Heppner had disclosed the information to a third party, Anthropic, whose terms of service permitted data collection and disclosure. Confidentiality — the cornerstone of privilege — was fatally compromised the moment he typed it into the consumer tool.

Dan: And on work product?

Tara: Also denied. Heppner created the documents on his own initiative, not at his counsel's direction. The documents didn't reflect counsel's legal strategy or mental impressions. They reflected Heppner's own thinking, processed through a commercial AI tool. but here's the critical aside from the court — and this is the part every lawyer should underline.

Dan: What did the court say?

Tara: Judge Rakoff noted the outcome might have been different if counsel had directed Heppner to use the AI tool. In that scenario, the tool might function as a lawyer's agent under the Kovel doctrine under US law — the same principle that protects communications with accountants or interpreters hired by counsel to assist with legal work. The distinction matters enormously. Counsel-directed use of AI, with proper confidentiality agreements in place, occupies fundamentally different legal ground than a client freelancing with a consumer chatbot.

Dan: So the tool isn't inherently privileged or not privileged — it depends on how it's used and under whose direction.

Tara: Precisely. And that brings us to the practical implications. using a consumer AI tool to analyse privileged information is functionally the same as discussing your case with a stranger in a public place. The convenience of the tool doesn't change the privilege analysis. The speed doesn't change it. The quality of the output doesn't change it. You've disclosed to a third party whose terms permit data use and disclosure. Privilege is gone.

Dan: what about enterprise tools? Are those safe?

Tara: Enterprise tools with proper confidentiality agreements are in a fundamentally different position — the provider has contractually committed to not using your data, not training on it, and maintaining confidentiality. but I have to note — this hasn't been fully tested in court yet. Heppner drew a bright line around consumer tools. The enterprise side is still an open question, though the legal logic is much stronger when you have a proper confidentiality agreement in place.

Dan: And clients need to know this?

Tara: Urgently. Many people — including sophisticated clients — treat AI chatbots as private conversations. They are not private. At least not on consumer tiers. Your clients need to be warned explicitly. If they're using AI tools to think through legal strategy, to analyse documents you've given them, to draft communications about their case — they could be waiving privilege without realising it. And once privilege is waived, it's gone.

Dan: so what's the framework? How should a lawyer evaluate any AI tool from a confidentiality perspective?

Tara: Four questions. First — read the terms of service. Specifically the data use, data retention, and disclosure provisions. Second — does the provider contractually commit to not training on your data? Third — does the provider contractually commit to confidentiality equivalent to what you'd expect from any professional service provider? And fourth — if the answers aren't clear, treat the tool as non-confidential. Full stop.

Dan: when you say "confidentiality equivalent to what you'd expect from a professional service provider" — what does that actually look like in practice?

Tara: You're looking for specifics. Does the provider offer enterprise-grade privacy controls — encryption, audit logs, access controls? Do they have independent security certifications like SOC 2 Type II or ISO 27001? What about identity management — single sign-on, multi-factor authentication? And critically — where is your data actually stored? Some clients have data residency requirements, meaning their data cannot leave a particular jurisdiction. If the provider's servers are overseas and your client's data can't leave Singapore or the EU, that's a problem you need to catch before you start using the tool, not after.

The tool you choose, and the contractual terms behind it, is now a privilege question, not just an IT question.
