Tara: It's not. And let me start with the golden rule. Assume everything you type into a cloud AI tool could be read by a human at the provider, subpoenaed by a regulator, or surfaced in a data breach. Act accordingly. Even with enterprise tools and contractual protections — minimise what you expose. The safest information is information you never shared in the first place.

Dan: that's a much more conservative posture than I think most people take.

Tara: It is. And it's the right one. So let's get specific. Rule number one — never input sensitive information directly. No client names. No party names. No deal names. No dates that identify specific transactions. No financial figures tied to identifiable entities. No privileged communications or legal strategy. And no personal data — national identification numbers, passport numbers, addresses, medical details. None of it goes in.

Dan: Okay, that list makes sense. But here's where I think it gets tricky — what about situations where you don't use a name but the facts are so specific that someone could figure it out?

Tara: And that is the one people miss. this is probably the most important thing I'll say today. You don't have to type "Client X" for the model — or for anyone who later reads your prompt — to know who you're talking about. If your prompt says "a Singapore-listed semiconductor company that recently received a takeover offer from a US private equity firm," there may be only one entity that fits that description.

Dan: I hadn't thought about that. You could be completely anonymous in terms of names but still be identifiable from the facts.

Tara: Exactly. This is the inference problem. And here's the test — if a reasonably informed person reading your prompt could identify the client, the matter, or the parties, you've put in too much. the solution is to sanitise aggressively. Change industries. Change geographies. Change numbers. Change party descriptions. Make the facts generic enough that they could describe dozens of situations, not just one.

Dan: that sounds like a lot of work. Is it really necessary every time?

Tara: Every time. And it gets easier with practice. Think of it like redacting a document before producing it in discovery — you wouldn't hand over an unredacted document and hope nobody notices the sensitive parts. Same principle here. Now, let me address the most common way sensitive data actually enters an AI tool. It's not lawyers carefully typing out confidential information. It's copy and paste.

Dan: The copy-paste trap.

Tara: a lawyer is working on a contract. They want the AI to improve a clause or suggest alternatives. So they highlight the paragraph, copy it, and paste it straight into the chat. That paragraph contains the party names, the deal value, the specific obligations — everything. It takes two seconds and it happens dozens of times a day across the profession.

Dan: And once it's pasted, it's gone — it's in the provider's system.

Tara: Right. So before you paste anything, ask yourself one question — does this contain any identifying information? If the answer is yes, strip it out first. Replace "Acme Corporation" with "Company A." Replace "forty-two million dollars" with "the purchase price." Replace Singapore with "the relevant jurisdiction." Redaction is not optional. It's the baseline.

Dan: what can you safely do? Because if I have to sanitise everything, it sounds like AI becomes almost unusable.

Tara: Not at all. There's a lot you can do safely. you can ask general legal questions — "what are the typical representations and warranties in a share purchase agreement?" That's textbook knowledge. You can provide fully anonymised fact patterns. You can paste sanitised contract clauses with all identifying details removed - there are tools for automated redaction of Personally Identifiable Information - also known as PII. You can ask the model to draft from scratch based on generic instructions. And you can use the model as a thinking partner for structure and approach without revealing the specifics of your matter.

Dan: So the model is still useful — you just need to be disciplined about what you feed it.

Tara: Exactly. And here's my practical recommendation — build the habit before you need it. Create a personal checklist. Before hitting send on any prompt, scan for names, numbers, dates, and identifiers. Some lawyers keep what they call a sanitisation template — a standard set of placeholder names and facts that they swap in before prompting. "Company A" and "Company B" instead of the real parties. "Country X" instead of the actual jurisdiction. Round numbers instead of specific figures. And if you want to go further, there are more sophisticated PII tools that let you round-trip the information — sanitise on the way in, desanitise on the way out.

Dan: and if your firm has an enterprise tool with contractual protections, the rules are more relaxed?

Tara: They may be — depending on the specific contractual terms. But even then, the habit of minimising exposure is good practice. Contracts can be breached. Providers can be hacked. Regulators can compel disclosure. The contractual protections add a layer of safety, but they don't make the underlying information invulnerable. Defence in depth — that's the principle.

Dan: So you treat the contract as one layer of protection, not the only layer.

Tara: Precisely. And let me close with an analogy. You wouldn't leave a client's confidential documents on a café table while you go to the bathroom, even if the café has CCTV. The security camera exists, but you still don't expose what you don't need to expose. Same logic applies to AI tools. The contractual protections exist, but you still sanitise. You still minimise. You still think before you paste.

Dan: Don't leave your documents on the table. I'll remember that one.

Tara: the takeaway is this — sanitise before you prompt, every single time. And remember that sensitive information isn't just names and numbers. It's anything from which someone could work backwards to identify the client or the matter. The inference problem is real, and the copy-paste trap catches more lawyers than any other vector. Build the habit now.
