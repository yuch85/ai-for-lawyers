Tara: exactly. Low risk — administrative tasks, meeting notes, brainstorming summaries. Medium risk — routine client updates, standard document review. High risk — court submissions, regulatory filings, commercial contracts, legal advice. And you match your oversight to that spectrum.

Supervisory checks, structured sampling, escalation when something looks off. This works for lower-risk tasks where outputs are easily reversible.

Tara: AI models reflect prejudices baked into their training data — including case law that disproportionately disadvantages certain groups, or datasets that underrepresent particular jurisdictions. Tara: No. It's an argument for being intentional about training. the goal isn't just teaching juniors how to use AI tools — it's developing them holistically into competent legal professionals. Tara: A crucial one. Seniors who supervise AI-assisted work need to understand what the tool can and cannot do. Your ethical duties haven't been rewritten by AI — but the context in which you apply them has changed fundamentally. Competence now means knowing how to use these tools and knowing how to catch their mistakes. Match your level of oversight to the stakes, watch for bias as well as hallucination, and remember that disclosure and transparency are fast becoming the professional norm, not the exception.
