I.

The Principle

Every box in COSTAR and GCES was context. The frameworks were teaching you what context feels like. Now you know. One rule from here.

II.

Example 1: Stream of Consciousness

A corporate lawyer dumps their thoughts like instructing senior counsel — a SAFE conversion, a shareholder cap problem, parentheticals as “show your working” instructions. No framework. Works.

III.

Example 2: One Sentence

A precise legal research query about privity of contract and third-party enforcement. No role, no tone, no audience. Seven words of pure context do more work than most paragraphs.

IV.

Example 3: The Conversation

Two prompts, not one. The lawyer tells the model to search for facts, then layers new information and asks for re-evaluation. Iteration — the technique no framework teaches.

Alice: Every box you filled — Goal, Context, Objective, Style, Tone, Audience, Expectations, Source — was context. All of it. You were supplying context the whole time. The frameworks were teaching you what context feels like.

Alice: Now you know. So you don't need the labels.

Alice: One rule from here. Context. Everything else was just learning the word for it.

Dan: So the last two episodes were a setup.

Alice: They were a genuine education. GCES works. COSTAR works. If you use them for the rest of your career, you'll write better prompts than most people. But the principle underneath both of them is simpler. Every box in both frameworks is just a way of saying: give the model what it needs to do the job. Context.

Dan: And the principle is that once you understand that, you don't always need every box.

Alice: Exactly. If the only audience is yourself, you don't set the Audience box. If the model can search for context, you don't paste it in. And you can iterate rather than perfecting a single prompt. The frameworks are training wheels. Useful — genuinely useful — but the expert move is knowing when to take them off.

Dan: Prove it. Show me prompts from a working lawyer that break the framework rules and still work.

Alice: Three of them.

Dan: Example one. This is a corporate lawyer instructing their AI the way you'd instruct senior counsel. Stream of consciousness.

Alice: Here's the prompt. "Act as a corporate lawyer who specialises in corporate finance. You are advising a pre-revenue company which has an upcoming equity round. The company has a legacy SAFE — explain what this is — that will convert on initial closing of the equity round. If SAFE conversion happens the company will have more than fifty shareholders, who are all small seed shareholders — explain why this will happen — which exceeds the maximum number of shareholders for a private company under the jurisdiction's companies legislation. The desire is to keep it under fifty shareholders even after the upcoming equity funding round. Client wants the simplest and lowest cost solution to solve this as the company has low cash flow until completion of the equity round."

Dan: No GCES structure. No COSTAR boxes. Just a brain dump.

Alice: And it works. Because the context is dense and the question is specific. Look at the parentheticals — "explain what this is," "explain why this will happen." The lawyer is asking the model to confirm its own understanding before proceeding. That's how you instruct senior counsel. You dump your thoughts, they're capable enough to parse them, and you ask them to show their working.

Dan: There's one piece of structure in there though. "Act as a corporate lawyer."

Alice: She kept the role because it adds context. She threw away everything else because it wouldn't have. That's the principle in action. Keep what adds context, discard what doesn't.

Dan: Example two. The opposite end of the spectrum. Precise, tight, no role, no formatting instructions. Just a question.

Alice: "Under common law jurisdictions where there is the concept of privity of contract, what is the purpose of a clause that allows one contracting party to enforce the agreement directly against the other contracting party's subcontractors, who are not parties to the contract?"

Dan: That's one sentence.

Alice: One sentence that does more work than most multi-paragraph prompts. Look at what's packed in there. The jurisdictional scope — common law. The conceptual anchor — privity of contract. The specific mechanism — third-party enforcement against subcontractors. And then the parenthetical: "who are not parties to the contract." Seven words that tell the model: I already know the tension here. I'm flagging it so you address it directly.

Dan: Adding COSTAR boxes to that prompt would make it worse.

Alice: It would add noise, not clarity. The prompt is already pure context. Every word is doing a job. Knowing when not to use the framework is part of the principle.

Dan: Example three. This one is different. It's a conversation — two prompts, not one. The lawyer is using the model's search capability to gather facts, then layering new information and asking for re-evaluation.

Alice: Prompt one. "Search for news about a major cruise operator's cruise being cancelled in a port city — this just happened. If a person flew into that city for the cruise and it got cancelled due to engine issues, what legal remedies does that person have against the operator for recovery of the air ticket prices?"

Dan: So the lawyer doesn't paste in the facts. They tell the model to go find them.

Alice: That's still context. It's just context the model fetches rather than context you supply. The principle holds — the model needs context to do the job. The source of that context can be you, or it can be a tool the model has access to.

Dan: Then prompt two.

Alice: "The cruise was cancelled after one day, but they gave a full refund plus one free night hotel stay. Evaluate in light of these facts."

Dan: New facts change the analysis.

Alice: And that's iteration. The prompt doesn't have to be complete on the first attempt. This is how lawyers actually think — facts arrive in layers, analysis adjusts. No framework teaches this. But the principle — context, context, context — naturally accommodates it. You just keep adding context until the answer is right.

Dan: So every one of those prompts broke the framework rules.

Alice: None of them suffered for it. They worked because the lawyer knew what context the model needed and supplied it — in whatever shape made sense. A brain dump. A single sentence. A two-step conversation. Three different shapes, one principle.

Dan: That's the principle. That's the only rule.

Alice: Next episode — what happens when you deliberately withhold context. Specifically, when you don't want to give the model your conclusion. That's interrogation mode. See you then.
